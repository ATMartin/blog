"Time to Think", remix

Alex Martin

I often have ideas so ethereal that I have to say them out loud to crystalize them. For me, verbalizing an idea can help me immediately see it’s defects and strengths much more clearly. -- @Masondesu

I'm seriously considering attending The Iron Yard, a programming bootcamp just down the street in Greenville. I've applied, and I'm now in that mode my darling wife often *tsks* me for where I "research". This is one of my specialties: searching out each and every tiny little thing that might be tangentially related to a subject I'm interested in, then throwing it all into my mental bucket to settle out while I consider it all. I tend to invest an inordinate amount of time in reading up so I can be "overprepared" (read: further overwhelmed) as I make decisions about my future.

In my Google-fu frenzy, I came across a really great blog post by Mason Stewart, one of the teachers (professors? code-ninjas?) up at TIY Greenville. The blog post is title "Time to Think", and it's worth reading. Seriously. Go read it now.

This post, and the quote above in particular, speaks to me on a fundamental level. My brain is constantly working. I think of something, and before I can write it down I'm thinking of something else. It's always been a double-edged sword for me, as I have a lot of difficulty focusing on a task but I can absorb and process new information fairly quickly. I remember being 6 and walking around my room talking to myself just to keep my thoughts from disappearing.

My mind is a constant din. At 50%, I'm working, considering what's for dinner, checking my mental calendar for forgotten birthdays or missed events. But at 100% I'm stepping through imaginary doors, living out lives I've never lived, planning businesses and programs and songs and questioning everything around me. It's rare that I want it amped up like that, so I've learned with time to drown it out with music, or deaden it with hours upon hours of Netflix. The reality is that my best thoughts come when it's quiet and I'm alone. On early days home from work, I can be found pacing wildly up and down the stairs, speaking everything that comes to mind so I can snatch the concepts from the air and continue working them out. When I was in college, but still sharing a home with my parents, I would go for drives. I learned all the back roads around my home during that time, and at 1 AM I'd slip out and drive through the country for an hour, just whispering to myself and occasionally remembering to watch for deer. It was never about the driving experience for me, or the placed I'd go. It was just the quiet, and the opportunity to really think in a way that made sense to me. The kinesthetics of driving just kept my body busy so my mind could work.

The concept of "time to think" is something I've never really qualified, but reading Mason's spin on it made me really think about how critical it's always been for me. I seem to find myself with less and less of it now. When I'm home alone, I'm worried about the cats fighting or the dog eating...uh...something. When I drive, I find myself worrying about my schedule or turning the radio up to drown out my neural drone. It's a shame to think that something that simple can be so easily forgotten in the mess & stress.

I've been focused for a few months on improving myself as a whole. Maybe my next step should be finding some good time to think and letting my brain go all the way up to 11. I'd like to think there's still some good stuff to be found in there.
